## The box that doesn't get unpacked

After seven moves across five countries, I've developed a reliable barometer for how settled I feel somewhere: the last box. You know the one. The box that's been moved three times and never fully opened. Half kitchen stuff you don't need. A tangle of cables. Something from a previous life that you can't throw away and don't know where to put.

For years, that box was the truest measure of my ambivalence about wherever I was. If it was open, I intended to stay. If it was lurking in the corner, I was already mentally planning the next place.

But I've noticed something more interesting than that. There are three objects I unpack within the first hour of arriving anywhere — before the practical stuff, before the cables, before the plates. Not because they're essential in any functional sense. Because they change the room.

## The first object: the cup

Not a mug. A specific ceramic cup — wide, grey-brown, slightly irregular where the potter's thumb pressed in. I bought it at a market in Oaxaca from someone who made it by hand. It holds about 200ml. It's the right size for coffee in the morning and for chamomile at night.

The cup travels in my carry-on, wrapped in a sock, because I'm more anxious about breaking it than anything else I own. In the past four years it has sat on a windowsill in Berlin, a kitchen counter in Lisbon, a bathroom shelf in Mexico City because that apartment had no kitchen storage, and three different Airbnbs I'd rather not remember.

What it does is simple: it makes the room mine. Not because it's beautiful, although it is. Because it contains my ritual. Every morning I make something and I pour it into this cup and the morning feels like my morning, regardless of what city I woke up in or what the ceiling looks like.

The object is just a carrier for the practice. But it matters that it's *this* object and not any object. The weight is right. The size is right. I know how it fits in my hand.

## The second object: the photograph

I have one photograph that I print and reprint whenever it gets too worn. It's from a birthday in London, early evening, a table of people I love. The light is warm. Everyone is mid-sentence or mid-laugh. It was taken before I knew I was moving, before any of this became a thing I do with my life.

It lives in a plain black frame, six inches by four. Goes on a bedside table or a desk — whatever surface is closest to where I sleep. In a hotel room this means propping it against the lamp. In a proper apartment it gets a shelf.

The photograph is not decoration. It's a reminder that I have a life that extends beyond the room I'm currently in — people who know me well, a past that's real, a future that will reconstitute itself when I land somewhere for longer than a few months.

Somewhere between moves, I stopped thinking of this as sentimentality. It's just honest. The disorientation of arriving somewhere new isn't only logistical. You lose your sense of your own continuity. You become briefly very present — which is good — but also briefly very thin. The photograph adds some thickness back.

## The third object: something with a smell

This changes. Currently it's a bar of soap from a perfumery in San Cristóbal de las Casas — dark, dense, smelling of copal and cedar. Before that it was a small cedar block. Before that, incense from a shop in Marrakech that I've since never been able to find again.

The logic is simple: smell is the most direct route to a felt sense of place. When I step into a room that smells of this soap, something in my body registers *home* before my brain gets there. It's almost embarrassingly effective.

I put it in the bathroom, or wherever the shower is. Not as a functional soap — I have functional soap for that. This one sits on the edge of the sink or on a shelf, and it does its job of making the air in that bathroom feel familiar.

## What three objects teach you

The thing I keep learning from this practice is that feeling at home is not about having enough stuff. It's not about having the right furniture or finding a place that looks like somewhere you'd want to live. It's about having a handful of consistent sensory inputs that your nervous system recognizes as *yours*.

Three objects is, it turns out, approximately the right number. Enough to mark a space without having to fill it. Enough to anchor you without requiring permanence. You're not pretending the borrowed apartment is yours. You're just making yourself genuinely present in it.

The last box can stay closed. The cup, the photograph, and the smell are enough to begin.
