## CDMX, 6:47am

There's a specific quality of morning light in Mexico City's Colonia Roma that I've never been able to replicate in my memory. It comes in low through the tall windows and lands on everything differently depending on the season — golden in October, flat and cool in February, almost green in the rainy weeks of July.

I lived there for eleven months. On my last morning, I stood in my kitchen making coffee and had the distinct feeling of being *at home* — not in a nostalgic way, not because I was leaving, but because I'd finally stopped waiting for it to feel permanent before I let myself settle in.

It was a rented apartment. The furniture wasn't mine. In three days I would be in Amsterdam.

And still: home.

## The five countries, briefly

I won't give you a life story. The countries are: South Africa (where I'm from), the UK, Germany, Mexico, and now — provisionally — Portugal. There have been shorter stints in Japan, the US, and Colombia, but those were extended visits, not attempts at living.

Each time I moved, I told myself the next place would be permanent. That this would be the city I finally stopped looking for something in. That I would build a life there in all the ways that word implies: community, routine, the kind of friendships where someone has a key to your place.

Some of those things happened. Some didn't. The city never turned out to be the variable that mattered.

## The object on the shelf

I have a small ceramic bowl I bought in a market in Berlin. It's not beautiful in any notable way — it's matte, low, slightly irregular. I use it to hold coins and keys and the occasional earring I've set down without thinking.

It's been in five countries. It's been on eleven windowsills.

When I unpack it in a new apartment, something shifts. Not immediately — but within a day or two, the space starts to feel like mine. The bowl doesn't do anything. It's not a talisman. But it carries continuity in a way that my phone and laptop — which have traveled everywhere — don't. It's non-functional enough that its only purpose is to say: *someone lives here.*

## The shift

At some point, I stopped thinking about home as a location I hadn't found yet and started thinking about it as a practice I could either do or fail to do.

The practice involves small, specific things: making my own coffee instead of going out for the first week, finding the morning light (there's always a best corner for it), cooking something that requires more than one pot. Learning the name of the street below mine. Noticing what I can hear at 3am.

None of this is revolutionary advice. But it's the difference between living somewhere and waiting to find out if you're going to live somewhere.

## What belonging actually feels like

I used to think belonging was something you received — from a place, from a community, from the accumulated years of living somewhere. Now I think it's something you practice into existence, incrementally, through acts so small they don't feel like acts at all.

The morning in CDMX, with the October light coming through the tall windows: I'd made that. Not the light. The comfort in it.

When I moved to Lisbon, I unpacked the ceramic bowl on the first day. I know what I'm doing now. I'm not waiting for home to happen to me.

I'm making it.
