## The list

These are not my most important possessions. They're not my most expensive ones. But they're the objects that, when unpacked, make a space shift from *somewhere I'm staying* to *somewhere I live*.

No devices. No clothes. Only objects.

---

**1. The ceramic bowl.**

Low, matte, slightly irregular — bought at a market in Berlin eight years ago. I use it for coins, keys, and whatever small thing I've set down without thinking. Its only purpose is to say that someone lives here.

**2. A single piece of incense or resin.**

I carry raw copal, though it's been palo santo in different chapters. The smell is the point: it's consistent and mine, which is exactly what a new space isn't yet.

**3. A linen pillowcase.**

Hotel pillowcases feel like hotel pillowcases. One linen pillowcase from home, even on a pillow I don't own, means the first night somewhere new is still mine.

**4. A book I'm not actively reading.**

Not the one I'm in the middle of — that goes in my bag. This one goes on the nightstand, cover up. It signals permanence in a way that feels disproportionate to the effort of carrying it.

**5. A candle, preferably with some hours already burned.**

A new candle in a new space feels performative. A half-burned one feels like mine. I choose unscented, because the copal is already doing the work of smell.

**6. A small photograph, printed.**

Not framed. Just a photograph I can tuck against a mirror or leave on a shelf. It doesn't have to be of people I love — though it often is. It just has to be a photograph I chose, not a screensaver.

**7. A kitchen knife worth using.**

This is the practical one. Cooking in a new place with someone else's dull knife is a specific kind of loneliness. My own knife means I can make a real meal on the first night, which is one of the fastest ways I know to feel human in an unfamiliar kitchen.

---

## Why objects

There's something I've noticed across many moves: the spaces that felt most like mine were not the ones I'd lived in longest, or the ones with the best light, or the ones I'd furnished most carefully.

They were the ones where I'd unpacked these seven things first.

I can't fully explain the mechanism. But I think it has something to do with intention — not in any abstract sense, but in the literal sense of the word: *to stretch toward*. When I unpack objects that belong to me, I'm declaring, in the smallest and most private way possible, that I intend to live here.

The space responds to that. Or maybe I do.

Either way, it works.
