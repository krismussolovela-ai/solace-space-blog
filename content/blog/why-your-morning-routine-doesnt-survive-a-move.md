## The routine that didn't survive the Atlantic

I had a very good morning routine in London. Coffee, then twenty minutes of reading before opening my phone, then a walk along the canal if it wasn't raining, which meant roughly three times a week. The whole thing took about forty-five minutes. I was attached to it in the way you get attached to things that work quietly and reliably.

Then I moved to Mexico City and none of it worked.

The coffee was different — better, actually, but different enough that it disrupted the sequence. There was no canal. The light came up earlier and more aggressively. The neighbourhood was loud in ways I wasn't used to. My body didn't know what time it was for about six weeks. The routine dissolved completely in the first fortnight, and what I was left with was just mornings. Unstructured, slightly anxious, full of phone-checking.

This is a familiar experience for anyone who moves regularly. The routine that took months to build in one context collapses almost immediately in a new one. And it's not weakness or bad habits — it's something structural about what routines actually are.

## Routines are location-dependent

The mistake I made for years was thinking that a routine was a sequence of actions. Wake, coffee, read, walk. If I just did those actions in the same order in the new place, I'd have the routine.

What I didn't understand is that a routine is less about the actions than about the cues. The canal was a cue — the specific light on the water at that hour, the cold air, the fact that I was physically outside and moving. The routine didn't survive the move because the cues didn't come with me. I tried to do the same actions without the same triggers, and it felt like going through motions.

The cues that make a routine feel like a ritual rather than a checklist are mostly environmental: the quality of light in a particular room at a particular hour, the sound of a specific street at that time of morning, the temperature of the air, the view from the window. Most of these are deeply local. They don't transfer.

## What does transfer

After enough moves, I've identified the elements of a morning that survive relocation:

**Sequence, not content.** What carries is not the specific actions but the specific order of a small number of actions. My current morning sequence is: water, then no phone for twenty minutes, then coffee, then one page of handwriting. The actions are less important than the fact that they happen in that order, every time. The sequence is portable in a way that the canal was not.

**One physical anchor object.** I've written elsewhere about the cup I carry everywhere. The cup goes on whatever surface is closest to where I make coffee, and the morning sequence begins when I reach for it. An anchor object makes the sequence recognizable to your body even when everything else — the room, the light, the sounds — is foreign.

**A time boundary, not a content boundary.** I've stopped trying to do particular things in the morning and started trying to protect a particular window of time. The first forty-five minutes after I wake up are mine in a specific way: no calls, no messages, no decisions. What I do in that window varies by city — sometimes I read, sometimes I write, sometimes I just sit with coffee — but the window stays consistent. This is easier to defend than a specific set of actions, because it doesn't require anything external to cooperate.

## The rebuilding process

When I arrive somewhere new, I don't try to reinstall the old routine immediately. That's the mistake. The new environment hasn't offered its cues yet, and you can't force them.

Instead, I do two things for the first two weeks.

First: I protect the anchor object. The cup goes on the surface. The sequence begins the same way it always begins, even if what follows is shorter or different than usual.

Second: I pay attention to when things feel easy. Is there a time in the morning when the light is particularly good in this apartment? A quality of quiet at a particular hour? A corner that feels like somewhere you'd want to sit with a notebook? These are the new cues offering themselves. When I notice one, I use it. I sit in that corner. I face that light. The routine starts to build itself around what the new place actually offers, rather than what I wished it offered.

After two or three weeks, I usually have something again — not the same as before, but genuinely mine. The sequence is recognizable. The anchor object is doing its job. The morning is mine.

## What you're actually building

The thing that makes a morning routine worth having isn't productivity or optimization. It's a felt sense of continuity — the recognition that *I am the same person who did this yesterday, in this room or another room, in this city or another city.* When you live between places, continuity can be genuinely hard to feel. The morning routine, at its best, is one of the places you feel it.

The canal in London was a beautiful cue and I miss it. But what I was reaching for via the canal — twenty minutes of physical movement, a particular quality of attention, the sense of having started the day on purpose — I have found again, in different forms, in every city since.

It just took two weeks and a cup.
