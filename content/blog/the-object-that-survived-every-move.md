## Eleven apartments

The ceramic bowl has been in eleven apartments. I know this because I counted — not deliberately, but because the number surfaced one evening in Lisbon while I was unwrapping it from a t-shirt I'd used as padding, and I found I could trace its entire itinerary from memory.

Berlin, where I bought it at a flea market in Prenzlauer Berg for four euros, from a man who didn't speak English and didn't need to. London, two apartments — Peckham first, then a room in Stoke Newington I left after six months without fully understanding why. Cape Town, a year that didn't become what I'd expected. Mexico City twice: two different neighbourhoods, two different versions of the same attempt to live there. Amsterdam, a sublease that was supposed to be temporary and was. And now Lisbon, an apartment with tall windows and the sound of fado from the restaurant below on Friday evenings.

Eleven apartments. Five countries. One bowl.

It is not beautiful in any notable way. Low, matte, ceramic, slightly irregular — the kind of object that takes up space on a shelf without announcing itself. I use it for coins, keys, the occasional earring set down without thinking. It has no function that couldn't be performed by a saucer or a jam jar lid.

But it has been in every room I've lived in for eight years, and that fact, I've come to understand, is itself the function.

## What attachment actually means

We use the word *attachment* loosely — usually as a mild problem to be solved. The suitcase as metaphor: you're dragging too much weight from the past, you need to set it down, travel light. There's an entire self-help genre premised on this. Declutter. Release. Let go.

But there is a different kind of attachment that has nothing to do with the past at all. It is about continuity — the unbroken thread running through all the rooms you've slept in, all the mornings you've made coffee in someone else's kitchen, all the windows that faced the wrong direction and gradually became right.

The bowl is that thread. It doesn't carry memory the way a photograph does — it doesn't represent anyone or remind me of a particular moment. It's not sentimental in the usual sense. What it carries is simpler: the fact of having been there. Of having been mine, in every room I have designated mine, for a long time.

When I unpack it in a new apartment, I'm not recreating the past. I'm establishing that this place belongs to the same story. The bowl says: *someone lives here.* More specifically, it says: this person lives here — the one who has had this object since Berlin, who bought it from a man who didn't speak her language on a Sunday morning in 2017.

That specificity matters more than I expected it to.

## The objects that don't travel

Not everything makes it.

In London I left behind a glass vase I'd had for three years because it didn't fit the bag and I thought I wouldn't miss it. I still think about that vase occasionally — not with grief exactly, but with the particular feeling of having let a thread drop. The apartment I moved to in Cape Town never quite felt finished. It took me months to understand why, and by the time I did, I was already planning to leave.

I've learned since then to be deliberate about what travels. Not because possessions are precious, but because continuity is. The objects that matter aren't the expensive ones or the meaningful ones in a narrative sense. They're the ones that have accumulated enough *presence* — enough rooms, enough mornings — to carry the thread forward into the next place.

A linen pillowcase from three apartments ago. A kitchen knife I've used for six years. A book that has been on my nightstand unread since Mexico City, that I keep because its presence is the point, not its content.

And the bowl. Always the bowl.

## What survives a move — and why

Here is what I've noticed about the object that survives every move: it rarely starts out as significant. It becomes significant through accumulation — through the sheer improbability of still being there.

Eight years ago I paid four euros for a bowl from a stranger at a Sunday market. I had no idea it would be in eleven apartments. I had no idea I would be in eleven apartments. I didn't choose it for its durability or its meaning. I chose it because I needed somewhere to put my keys and it was four euros and it looked right.

But it survived the cull of every departure. The weight limit of every airline. The judgment call of every packing session: *does this come?* And each time it came, it became slightly more irreplaceable — not because of what it represents, but because of how long it has been present.

That is what I mean by attachment in the useful sense. Not the grip of the past, but the weight of accumulated presence. The thing that has been in the room long enough to be part of what the room is.

## The question worth asking before the next move

Before the next move — whenever it comes — I'll do what I always do: stand in the apartment and decide what travels. The question I've stopped asking is *what is this worth?* or *will I have room for it?*

The question I ask now is simpler: *how long has this been mine, and can I afford to let that thread drop?*

For the bowl, the answer has been the same eleven times.

It comes.
