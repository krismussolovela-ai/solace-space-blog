## How I arrived

I moved to Mexico City in late autumn, on a flight that landed at six in the morning. By eight I was in the apartment I'd rented sight unseen, standing in the kitchen of a Colonia Roma ground floor with high ceilings and a window onto a small courtyard where a bougainvillea was doing something spectacular. I made instant coffee — my good coffee hadn't arrived yet — and stood at the window watching the courtyard and thinking: this is it.

I had moved for reasons I had told myself were practical. Lower cost of living, a new creative context, the specific light I'd heard about from other writers and hadn't quite believed until now. I had a rough plan: three months minimum, six if it felt right. I had work to do. I had a desk.

What I didn't have, and didn't know I needed, was a different relationship with time.

## The first weeks

Mexico City is a city with a particular talent for disrupting Northern European temporal logic. Things take longer than you expect and also happen more abruptly. A lunch that was meant to take an hour takes three, and this is not considered an overage — it is considered a success. A delivery arrives four days late and then again four hours later, twice. Plans are made and remade with a fluidity that would be chaos in London and is simply Tuesday here.

I spent the first three weeks fighting it. Trying to find the rhythm I was used to — the predictable start and end of things, the day divided into reliable blocks. I had a good friend who had lived in the city for years and watched me do this with the patient amusement of someone who had been through it herself.

*You're still on European time*, she said one Sunday, as I was visibly anxious about the fact that we'd been sitting in this restaurant for two hours and hadn't yet ordered dessert and I had things to do at home. *Not jet lag. The other kind.*

## What I learned to do instead

The shift happened gradually, and I couldn't tell you the exact moment it began. But I remember noticing one afternoon in December that I had spent two hours in the Mercado Medellín without looking at my phone. I had walked through it slowly, looked at things, bought a single copal resin block and a small jar of dried hibiscus, and talked to the person selling it for longer than any information exchange strictly required.

I had done this without thinking. And I had felt, on the walk home, something adjacent to satisfaction that was not about having accomplished anything.

This is what the city was teaching me: that presence and productivity are not the same thing, and that I had spent years confusing them.

In practice, this translated into specific changes. I stopped working through lunch. I started taking the long way, often. I began treating the afternoon coffee break — a real one, seated, not at my desk — as non-negotiable rather than optional. I started arriving at things at the time I actually expected to arrive rather than the optimistic time I had told myself I would leave by.

None of this made me less productive. If anything the opposite. But more importantly: it made the days feel longer in the right way. Fuller.

## The copal in the mornings

The resin I'd bought at the market became part of my morning ritual. I'd light a small piece of it on a clay dish while the coffee brewed, and the smell — dense, resinous, slightly sweet — would drift through the apartment in the way that incense does if you burn very little of it. Not overwhelming. Just present.

The smell of copal in the morning in Mexico City is one of those sensory experiences I associate now with a particular quality of time. Slow time. Not empty, not wasted — just unhurried. The opposite of the feeling that you should already be somewhere else doing something else.

I brought a block of it back. It sits on my desk now, wherever my desk is. I don't always light it — sometimes just the smell of the cold resin is enough. But when I do, I remember what it felt like to spend a morning in that apartment in Roma, with the bougainvillea doing its spectacular thing in the courtyard, not in a hurry.

## What stayed

I live differently since Mexico City. Not dramatically — not in ways that would be legible to someone who only saw the surface. But in my own rhythm I notice the difference.

I take the long way more. I build more dead time into the day and call it something other than wasted. I sit at meals. When I'm waiting for something — a delayed friend, a slow coffee, a queue — I wait instead of filling the waiting. This sounds small. I think it's actually large.

The city taught me that slowness is not the absence of speed. It's a different thing entirely — a different relationship with duration, a different way of measuring whether a day was good. *Did I do everything I meant to?* is a European question. The Mexican question, or at least the question the city kept asking me, was simpler: *Was I actually there?*

I'm still working on the answer.
