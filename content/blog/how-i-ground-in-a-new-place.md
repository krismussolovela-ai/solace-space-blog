## The moment I started noticing arrival

I used to walk into a new room and immediately open my laptop. It was a kind of deflection — if I was already working, I didn't have to reckon with the fact that I was somewhere unfamiliar, that the ceiling was wrong, that the light came in from the wrong side.

I changed that in Oaxaca, during a residency I'd booked impulsively. My room had white walls, a single window that faced a courtyard, and a smell I couldn't name — something between dried herbs and old wood. I sat on the edge of the bed with my bag still on my back and thought: *I don't know how to be here yet.*

That thought was the beginning of something useful.

## The ritual (in order)

What I do now takes under ten minutes. I do it every time — a rented apartment, a friend's spare room, a hotel in a city I don't speak the language of.

**Step one: put the copal down.**

I carry a small chunk of raw copal resin. Not because it's spiritual, but because the smell is specific and consistent, and consistency is what the nervous system actually needs when everything else is foreign. I set it on whatever surface will become my desk or bedside table. I don't light it immediately. The act of placing it is enough — it marks the first corner of something familiar in an unfamiliar room.

If you don't have copal, a stone works. Anything that has traveled with you and holds no meaning in this new place except the meaning *you* bring to it.

**Step two: open every window, even briefly.**

This is about claiming the air. Five breaths of whatever the outside smells like. Rain on pavement in Prague, jasmine in Medellín, salt in Lisbon. You are learning the atmosphere. You are telling your body: this is where we are now.

**Step three: find where the light is best at this hour.**

Don't consult a map. Walk the space — even if it's a single room — and find where you'd want to sit at this exact time of day. Make note of it. You'll use it tomorrow.

**Step four: unpack one non-essential thing.**

Not your toiletries, not your charger. One object that belongs on a surface: a candle, a photograph, a book you're not even reading. The point is to interrupt the impermanence. A bag packed is a declaration that you might leave at any moment. An object on a shelf says something different.

**Step five: make something to drink.**

Whatever the kitchen or kettle allows. Tea, hot water with lemon, coffee. The ritual of making a drink is its own arrival. You have used the space. You have decided to be here.

## Why it works

None of this is mystical. It's sequential and sensory, and that's the point. Your nervous system doesn't understand "I am traveling" as a category of experience — it understands inputs: smell, temperature, light, sound. When you give it consistent inputs in a new place, you are building a bridge between the familiar and the foreign.

Ten minutes is enough. You don't have to love the room. You don't have to write anything down. You just have to arrive before you start doing everything else.

The copal helps. But it's really just an anchor.
